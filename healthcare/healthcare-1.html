PAYING FOR HEALTHCARE IN CAMBODIA
Why it matters: Despite making enormous progress against communicable diseases, child mortality, maternal health, and access to hygiene through proper water supply and sanitation systems, Cambodia still faces major hurdles in the healthcare sector. Chief among these issues is accessibility: the lack of a developed health insurance ecosystem means the share of out of pocket expenditures remains too high, many Cambodian are unable to afford proper healthcare and health expenses are a drag on the budget of many others.
  • Rising health spending: Cambodia spends roughly 6% of its GDP annually on healthcare, a figure that has not changed much throughout the last two decades. Because the country’s GDP is rising fast, of course, it means health expenditure is also rising at a remarkable pace – from just $20 USD per year and per capita to $91 in 2018, according to publicly available estimates verified with World Bank Data. In purchasing-power-parity terms, this amounts to a roughly 2.5x increase in twenty years.
  • Share of out-of-pocket expenditures: The problem is that much of that money keeps coming out of the pockets of individuals and families. There has been a distinct rise in the share of public social health insurance in recent years with the rollout of the National Social Security Fund, but out-of-pocket expenditures still represent between 55 and 60% of total health spending, whereas just around 1% of the population benefits from private insurance coverage.
  • A question of financial literacy: It would make sense for many Cambodian families to get private health insurance. The market already provides quality coverage at reasonable prices that are financially accessible to most of the working population. But many of those who could get covered do not, because they do not understand insurance products or the concept of risk pooling and think they will do better financially by handling the matter themselves. Even top private hospitals in Phnom Penh catering to the rich see most patients pay directly without either private or public insurance.
  • Parallel systems: Many large companies are aware of the problem and see providing health benefits as a key part of employee retention incentivization schemes. These who have thousands of employees in particular can form large risk pools allowing for predictable costs (and sometimes even profit as many employees do not use the benefit) and relatively little risk since the scope of covered care and maximum annual payout is limited. In the countryside, there are no such formal structures, but villagers will appeal for help from the extended family when they meet unexpected health expenditures, which amounts to a form of informal risk pooling, but is not adequate and incentivizes not seeking care until it becomes absolutely necessary, when medical interventions are riskier, less effective, and more costly.
  • Payment Technologies: The speed of development of the insurance sector in Cambodia also depend on the development of the payments ecosystem – not only between insurance funds and companies, but also between consumers and insurance funds themselves: direct debit is not yet widely available, which forces consumers to pay insurance premiums on a yearly or quarterly basis.
  • Key Numbers:
    o Health expenditure as a share of GDP: 6%
    o Total expenditure on health: ~1.5 billion dollars
    o Private expenditure on health as a share of total health expenditure: 58.2%

The double burden of diseases and life expectancy in Southeast Asia
