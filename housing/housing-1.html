THE DECOUPLING BETWEEN HOUSING NEEDS AND REAL ESTATE MARKET VALUE IN PHNOM PENH
Why It Matters: Demand in real estate market is typically derived: it originates from demand for the physical good or service that can be produced or sold on a piece of land or in a building. Residential land is demanded for the dwellings it can support. Since housing, whether owner or renter-occupied, makes up the largest part of the urban fabric, demand for housing is usually a key determinant of real estate value in a given market. But because of a series of factors, this is no longer the case in much of Phnom Penh, and this will pose challenges to the city’s development in the next few decades.
  • The 2000s Land Boom: The 2001 Land law, granting ownership rights according to land occupation and titles recognized by provincial authorities, is passed and opens up the way for more private investment in real estate. Newfound political and monetary stability after the civil war finally ended created a particularly attractive scenario for cash-flush ASEAN investors making a long-term bet on the country. As the country was still very poor, land prices in Phnom Penh were extremely low and these investors correctly reasoned that prices were bound to increase as the country’s economy would develop, and land prices began to significantly rise.
  • The 2010s Condo Boom: Legislation passed in the late 2000’s allowed for foreign private ownership of strata titles above the first floor in condominium buildings. The following decade then saw an extraordinary rise in the number of condominiums erected in the capital. According to CBRE research, there were less than 5,000 condominium units in the city in 2016, whereas there are now more than 25,000, a 5x increase in just five years. Interestingly, despite perceptions that the condominium market is only geared at the wealthy, the fastest-growing condominium segment in recent years has been low-quality condominiums.
  • The Legacy of History: Because the contemporary distribution of private property in Phnom Penh is a direct legacy of the 1980s, when those who moved to the capital were granted housing and land on a first-come, first-served basis, rates of homeownership in Phnom Penh are very high. Some families with very little income stand on considerable (theoretical) land wealth but are used to living without much income and think they will profit more by waiting further to sell, straining local land markets liquidity and supply and generating further upwards pressure on land prices.
  • The Legacy of Culture: Cambodia was for thousands of years and until very recently an agriculture-based economy. Owning and acquiring land is therefore traditionally an important value in Khmer culture- as well as an important financial tool for families in rural areas, whether it be because the land can be farmed and put to good use or as a collateral for borrowing. The cultural premium on land will likely diminish over time, but only very slowly, and we expect it will continue to be a significant factor in analysis of Cambodian land prices for decades to come.
  • Sky-High Land Prices: Land rise in the early 2000s was compounded by the construction boom of the 2010s, and land prices in some prime streets in the capital now command prices that are similar to these of prime real estate in alpha cities of the developed world. This sets up a difficult background for the coming decades, because housing need will continue to rise in Phnom Penh but the high price of land will make it difficult for developers to build products that are both affordable to the local middle-class and generate enough profits to be worth the effort.
  • Financialization and Submarkets: The key reason behind the decoupling between Phnom Penh’s real estate market value and local housing demand is that the real estate market in Phnom Penh is no longer a single market, but has decoupled into several separate submarkets. The one everybody usually refers to in discussions of Phnom Penh real estate is a financialized market: heavy foreign investment have tied trends in this market to global capital flows and value is now tied to global financial dynamics rather than housing usage dynamics, driving prices upward.
  • Supply and Demand Mismatch: In the end, the main challenge in Phnom Penh is not that there is not enough supply to meet new demand, but rather that the new supply does not meet housing demand. This is partly because much of the new supply was never meant to meet local housing demand, and because a great many projects were built without the proper market research needed to understand local needs.
  • Ghost Buildings? A walk in downtown Phnom Penh at night amidst the tall, almost completely unlit new condo buildings is enough to see that most of these new dwellings have yet to find tenants. This is because they remain vastly unaffordable, either as a purchase or on a rental basis, for the vast majority of the local public. Even low-quality condominiums are too expensive by orders of magnitude for at least 80% of local buyers.
